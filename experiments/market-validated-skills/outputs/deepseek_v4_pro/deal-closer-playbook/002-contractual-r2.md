## Objection Playbook

### Known Objection: “Integration effort may be too high”

- **Fact**: Jordan Lee raised this.
- **Inference**: The objection likely comes from a perceived technical burden, lack of internal resources, or past integration pain. It may originate from a technical evaluator not yet identified.
- **Response approach (internal)**: Do not concede a “light” effort without proof. Gather specifics first.
  - **Ask**: “Can you walk me through what ‘too high’ looks like? Is it about API documentation, data formats, mapping to existing dispatch systems, or developer time?”
  - **Provide**: Technical integration artifacts (API docs, sample code, sandbox environment, typical integration timelines from similar logistics customers). Offer a joint technical session.
  - **Show**: Case studies of companies similar in size/logistics profile where integration was completed within X days/weeks (only if those examples exist and are publicly shareable or de‑identified).
- **Action**: Schedule a technical deep‑dive with the evaluation/IT owner. Pre‑empt by delivering a “getting‑started guide” and a sample integration plan. Identify the technical blocker.
- **Human gate**: Do not promise a specific integration timeline or level of effort without engineering input. Do not claim specific integration outcomes for Acme’s environment.

### Anticipated Objections (Inferences)

1.  **Objection**: “We’re already evaluating other solutions.”
    - **Inference**: Competitors unknown; need discovery.
    - **Action**: Ask directly: “What other approaches are you considering? How do you weigh factors like speed, accuracy, and total cost of ownership?” Map comparison points.
2.  **Objection**: “No budget this quarter.”
    - **Risk**: If deal size is unknown, this could surface.
    - **Action**: Position the business case by quantifying routing inefficiencies (fuel, driver time, late deliveries) from a discovery call. Build value hypotheses, but don’t promise ROI figures without their data.
