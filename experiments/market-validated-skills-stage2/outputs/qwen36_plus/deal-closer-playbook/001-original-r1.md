## 4. Objection Playbook

### Known Objection Responses

```
OBJECTION: "IT Security review is blocking us. We need to pass their compliance checks before we can expand."

WHY THEY SAY THIS:
IT is risk-averse. They fear data exposure, integration vulnerabilities, or audit failures. They will default to "no" if documentation is incomplete or if the vendor looks risky.

RESPONSE FRAMEWORK:
1. Acknowledge: "Completely understand. Security and compliance are non-negotiable, especially in manufacturing."
2. Reframe: "We've cleared this exact review with [X] similar manufacturers. We treat security as a partnership, not a hurdle."
3. Evidence: Provide SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, data residency docs, pre-filled security questionnaire, and architecture diagram.
4. Confirm: "If we get these to your IT lead by [Date] and schedule a 30-min technical walkthrough, does that unblock the timeline?"

EXAMPLE RESPONSE:
"I hear you, and I respect that IT needs to protect the environment. We've already passed security reviews with [Similar Manufacturer] and [Another Client]. I'll send over our full security packet, including our SOC 2 report, data flow architecture, and a pre-filled vendor questionnaire. Can we schedule a 30-minute call with your IT lead next week to walk through it? If we clear their checklist, are we aligned to move forward by [Target Date]?"

IF THEY PUSH BACK:
Offer a limited-scope pilot or phased rollout that isolates data to a non-production environment until IT signs off. Escalate to your internal Security/Compliance lead to join the call.
```

#### Anticipated Objections

**Price/Budget:** "We need to justify the $80k expansion."
- *Response:* Tie to cost of manual spreadsheet work. "At $80k, this replaces ~[X] hours/month of manual reconciliation and reduces compliance risk. The ROI model shows payback in [Y] months. Let's review it together."

**Timing:** "We're too busy this quarter to implement."
- *Response:* "We can phase the rollout. Start with core modules now, add advanced features post-quarter. I'll map a low-lift implementation plan that requires <2 hrs/week from your team."

**Status Quo:** "Spreadsheets work fine for now."
- *Response:* "They work until they don't. Version control breaks, audit trails disappear, and scaling adds hidden labor costs. We automate the heavy lifting so your team focuses on ops, not data entry."

**Risk:** "What if the expansion disrupts our current workflow?"
- *Response:* "We run a parallel validation period. Your current process stays live until we confirm data sync and user adoption. Zero downtime, full rollback option."

## 5. Competitive Positioning

```
COMPETITOR: Incumbent Spreadsheet Workflow (Excel/Google Sheets)

THEIR LIKELY PITCH:
Zero cost, fully customizable, already embedded in team habits, no new vendor onboarding, immediate availability.

WHERE THEY WIN:
Familiarity, flexibility, no procurement friction, instant deployment.

WHERE WE WIN:
Automated data validation, real-time collaboration, audit trails, role-based access control, API integrations, compliance readiness, scalability without manual overhead.

LANDMINE QUESTIONS:
1. "When your team shares the master spreadsheet, how do you track who changed what and when?"
2. "How many hours per week does your team spend reconciling version conflicts or fixing broken formulas?"
3. "If an auditor requests a 12-month change log for this process, how long would it take to compile from your current sheets?"

IF THE PROSPECT FAVORS THE COMPETITOR:
Do not attack spreadsheets. Acknowledge their utility for ad-hoc work. Position your platform as the "system of record" for scaled, auditable, cross-functional processes. Offer a migration template that imports their existing sheets to reduce switching friction.

TRAP TO AVOID:
Do not say "spreadsheets are bad." Say "spreadsheets are great for drafting, but risky for scaling." Focus on governance, not features.
```
