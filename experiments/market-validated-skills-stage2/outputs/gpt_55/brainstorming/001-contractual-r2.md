## Understanding Summary

**Fact:** The founder’s idea is: “We need an AI workspace where teams can co-edit customer proposals faster.”

**Interpreted opportunity:** A collaborative document workspace focused on helping teams create, revise, approve, and personalize customer-facing proposals with AI assistance.

**Current state:** Unknown. We do not yet know whether the team already uses tools like Google Docs, Notion, Microsoft Word, Salesforce, HubSpot, PandaDoc, DocuSign, or proposal/RFP software.

**Proposed outcome of this brainstorming:** Explore possible users, product directions, assumptions, and a lightweight validation path — without implementing anything.

---

## Possible User Segments

### 1. B2B Sales Teams

**Users:** Account executives, sales managers, sales engineers, revenue operations.

**Pain points:**
- Proposals take too long to draft.
- Reps reuse stale or inconsistent content.
- Legal, finance, product, and technical teams slow down approvals.
- Personalization is often shallow because of time pressure.

**AI value:**
- Generate first drafts from CRM notes, call transcripts, pricing inputs, and templates.
- Suggest tailored value propositions by customer industry or pain point.
- Flag missing sections, inconsistent claims, or outdated boilerplate.

**Attractiveness:** High. Sales teams directly connect proposal speed to revenue.

---

### 2. Agencies and Consultancies

**Users:** Strategy consultants, marketing agencies, software agencies, professional services firms.

**Pain points:**
- Teams create many custom proposals.
- Proposal quality varies by team member.
- Prior work, case studies, and pricing models are scattered.
- Collaboration across sales, delivery, and leadership is messy.

**AI value:**
- Turn discovery notes into polished proposals.
- Recommend relevant case studies.
- Adapt tone and scope by client size, industry, and budget.
- Help generate timelines, deliverables, and project plans.

**Attractiveness:** High, especially for small and mid-sized firms where proposal quality affects win rate.

---

### 3. Enterprise RFP / Procurement Response Teams

**Users:** Proposal managers, sales engineers, compliance teams, legal teams.

**Pain points:**
- RFPs are long, repetitive, and deadline-driven.
- Answers need to be accurate, approved, and compliant.
- Knowledge is spread across docs, wikis, past proposals, and SMEs.
- Collaboration is complex and high-stakes.

**AI value:**
- Draft responses from approved knowledge bases.
- Identify unanswered requirements.
- Route sections to subject-matter experts.
- Check compliance language and required attachments.

**Attractiveness:** Strong willingness to pay, but higher complexity and trust requirements.

---

### 4. Customer Success / Renewal Teams

**Users:** Customer success managers, account managers, renewal specialists.

**Pain points:**
- Renewal proposals and expansion plans require customer-specific context.
- Teams need to summarize usage, ROI, business outcomes, and next steps.
- Documents often require input from sales, support, and product.

**AI value:**
- Generate renewal briefs from customer health data and account notes.
- Suggest expansion narratives based on product usage.
- Create QBR-style proposals or mutual success plans.

**Attractiveness:** Medium to high, especially in SaaS companies.

---

### 5. Startups and Founder-Led Sales Teams

**Users:** Founders, early sales hires, operators.

**Pain points:**
- No mature sales enablement process.
- Founders manually craft every proposal.
- Speed matters, but quality must remain high.
- Institutional knowledge lives in founder conversations.

**AI value:**
- Convert call notes into credible proposals.
- Create reusable proposal templates.
- Help non-sales team members produce professional customer docs.

**Attractiveness:** Good early adopter segment, but potentially lower willingness to pay than enterprise sales teams.

## Alternative Product Directions

### Option A: AI-Native Collaborative Proposal Editor

A dedicated workspace where teams write, comment, co-edit, approve, and export customer proposals.

**Core concept:**
- Like Google Docs or Notion, but purpose-built for sales proposals.
- AI assists with drafting, rewriting, summarizing, and personalization.
- Includes proposal templates, reusable content blocks, comments, approvals, and version history.

**Best for:**
- Agencies, consultancies, startups, and mid-market B2B sales teams.

**Pros:**
- Clear product surface.
- Easy to understand.
- Can own the end-to-end proposal workflow.
- Strong opportunity for differentiated UX.

**Cons:**
- Hard to replace existing document habits.
- Users may resist another workspace.
- Needs excellent collaboration UX to compete with mature tools.

---

### Option B: AI Proposal Assistant Inside Existing Tools

An AI layer that works with existing tools such as Google Docs, Microsoft Word, Notion, Salesforce, HubSpot, or Slack.

**Core concept:**
- Instead of creating a new workspace, meet users where they already work.
- AI helps draft, rewrite, pull approved content, and personalize proposals inside current workflows.

**Best for:**
- Teams already standardized on existing document and CRM systems.

**Pros:**
- Lower adoption friction.
- Easier to position as workflow enhancement rather than replacement.
- Faster initial validation if delivered as an add-on, plugin, or assistant.

**Cons:**
- Less control over the user experience.
- Integration complexity.
- Platform dependency risk.
- Harder to build a defensible workspace over time.

---

### Option C: Proposal Knowledge and Reuse Engine

A system that helps teams find, reuse, and govern approved proposal content.

**Core concept:**
- Centralize prior proposals, case studies, pricing language, legal clauses, product descriptions, and compliance answers.
- AI recommends the best reusable content for each customer situation.

**Best for:**
- Enterprise sales, RFP teams, regulated industries, and larger revenue organizations.

**Pros:**
- Addresses a painful operational bottleneck.
- Strong value in consistency, compliance, and accuracy.
- Could become a system of record for customer-facing content.

**Cons:**
- Requires trust, permissions, and content governance.
- More complex onboarding.
- Value depends on quality of existing knowledge base.
- Less immediately exciting than “AI writes the proposal for you.”

---

## Recommended Initial Direction

**Recommended direction:** Start with **Option B: AI Proposal Assistant Inside Existing Tools**, focused on a narrow segment such as agencies, consultancies, or B2B sales teams.

**Rationale:**
- The founder’s phrase “co-edit customer proposals faster” suggests speed and collaboration are central.
- Most teams already have a document workflow, so replacing it immediately may be too much friction.
- An assistant that improves proposal creation inside existing workflows is easier to validate.
- If the assistant proves valuable, the product can later expand into a dedicated workspace or knowledge platform.

A possible positioning:

> “An AI proposal copilot that turns sales notes, templates, and team input into polished customer proposals — without forcing your team to leave its existing document workflow.”

## Key Assumptions

### Customer and Market Assumptions

1. **Assumption:** Teams perceive proposal creation as a meaningful bottleneck.
2. **Assumption:** Faster proposals improve win rates, sales velocity, or team productivity.
3. **Assumption:** Existing tools are too generic for proposal-specific collaboration.
4. **Assumption:** Teams are willing to pay for speed, quality, consistency, or compliance improvements.
5. **Assumption:** The best early adopters are teams producing frequent, semi-custom proposals.

---

### Product Assumptions

1. **Assumption:** AI-generated first drafts are useful enough to save meaningful time.
2. **Assumption:** Users want AI assistance, but still need human review and control.
3. **Assumption:** Proposal content can be safely reused from prior proposals, templates, and approved language.
4. **Assumption:** Collaboration features matter because proposals require input from multiple stakeholders.
5. **Assumption:** The product must support version history, comments, permissions, and approval workflows eventually.

---

### Data and Trust Assumptions

1. **Assumption:** Teams will allow customer proposal data to be processed by AI if privacy and permissions are clear.
2. **Assumption:** Accuracy matters more than creativity for many customer-facing proposals.
3. **Assumption:** Users need citations, source links, or “based on approved content” indicators to trust AI outputs.
4. **Assumption:** Sensitive information such as pricing, legal terms, and customer data requires careful access control.

---

### Go-to-Market Assumptions

1. **Assumption:** The buyer may be a sales leader, agency owner, founder, or revenue operations leader.
2. **Assumption:** The daily user may be an account executive, consultant, proposal manager, or sales engineer.
3. **Assumption:** Time saved per proposal is an easy initial ROI metric.
4. **Assumption:** Win rate improvement is attractive but harder to prove early.

## Risks

### Risk: Too Broad a Product

“AI workspace for teams” is broad and could become unfocused. The product needs a sharply defined wedge.

**Mitigation:** Start with one segment and one high-frequency workflow, such as turning sales call notes into a first proposal draft.

---

### Risk: Existing Tools Are Good Enough

Teams may already be comfortable with Google Docs, Microsoft Word, Notion, or proposal software.

**Mitigation:** Position around a painful job those tools do not solve well: proposal personalization, reuse of approved content, stakeholder coordination, or RFP response speed.

---

### Risk: AI Output Is Not Trusted

If AI generates inaccurate claims, outdated pricing, or incorrect capabilities, users may reject it.

**Mitigation:** Emphasize human review, source-backed suggestions, approved content libraries, and clear confidence boundaries.

---

### Risk: Collaboration Is Not the Main Bottleneck

The founder assumes co-editing is the key issue, but the real bottleneck may be discovery quality, pricing approval, legal review, or content retrieval.

**Mitigation:** Validate the full proposal workflow before committing to collaboration-heavy product design.

## Short Validation Path

### Step 1: Interview 10–15 Target Users

Focus on teams that create frequent customer proposals.

Suggested interview targets:
- 5 agency or consultancy leaders
- 5 B2B sales reps or sales leaders
- 3 proposal/RFP managers
- 2 founders doing founder-led sales

Key questions:
- How many proposals do you create per month?
- How long does each proposal take?
- Who contributes to the proposal?
- Where do delays happen?
- What parts are repetitive?
- What parts require judgment?
- What tools do you use today?
- What happens when a proposal is late or low quality?
- Would you trust AI to draft, edit, or recommend content?
- What would make this worth paying for?

---

### Step 2: Map the Proposal Workflow

Document the current process from opportunity to sent proposal.

Look for:
- Trigger: What starts a proposal?
- Inputs: CRM notes, call transcripts, templates, pricing, case studies.
- Collaborators: Sales, solutions, delivery, finance, legal, leadership.
- Bottlenecks: Drafting, review, pricing, approval, formatting.
- Output: PDF, Doc, deck, e-signature flow, email.
- Success metric: Time saved, fewer revisions, faster send time, better win rate.

---

### Step 3: Test Three Concept Prototypes

Use no-code mockups, clickable prototypes, or even manually generated sample outputs.

Concepts to test:

1. **Draft Generator**
   - “Upload notes or paste a call transcript, and get a proposal draft.”

2. **Approved Content Recommender**
   - “AI suggests relevant case studies, scope language, and reusable sections.”

3. **Collaborative Review Workspace**
   - “Assign proposal sections, request approvals, and track what is ready to send.”

Measure:
- Which concept gets the strongest reaction?
- Which saves the most time?
- Which solves the most painful step?
- Which would users pay for first?

---

### Step 4: Run a Concierge Pilot

Before building software, manually help 3–5 teams create proposals faster using AI and structured templates.

Track:
- Time from proposal request to first draft.
- Number of revisions.
- Number of collaborators involved.
- Quality rating from the team.
- Whether the customer-facing proposal was actually sent.
- Whether the team asks to use it again.

---

### Step 5: Define the Wedge

Choose one initial promise, for example:

- “Create a first proposal draft from sales notes in 10 minutes.”
- “Reuse approved proposal content without searching old docs.”
- “Coordinate proposal reviews across sales, legal, and delivery.”
- “Respond to RFP questions using approved company knowledge.”

The wedge should be narrow, frequent, painful, and measurable.

---

## Early Success Metrics

Potential metrics:
- Time to first draft reduced by 50% or more.
- Proposal turnaround time reduced by 30% or more.
- Number of stakeholder review cycles reduced.
- Percentage of AI-generated content accepted by users.
- Reuse rate of approved content.
- Weekly active proposal creators.
- Number of proposals created per account per month.
- Willingness to pay after pilot.
