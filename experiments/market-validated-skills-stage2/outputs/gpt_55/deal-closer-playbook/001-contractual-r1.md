## 7. Competitive Positioning

### Competitor: Incumbent Spreadsheet Workflow

This is not a traditional software competitor; it is a **status quo competitor**.

| Spreadsheet Workflow Strength | Counter-Positioning |
|---|---|
| Familiar to users | Familiarity does not equal scalability, governance, or reliability |
| Low visible cost | Hidden costs include manual labor, errors, rework, reporting delays, and lack of auditability |
| Flexible | Flexibility can create inconsistency, version-control issues, and process variation |
| No procurement/security friction | Continuing with spreadsheets may avoid vendor review but can preserve operational risk |
| Already adopted | Existing use does not prove it supports expansion, visibility, or cross-team execution |

### Recommended Positioning Theme

**Move the conversation from “Why replace spreadsheets?” to “What is the cost and risk of continuing to run critical operations through spreadsheets?”**

### Value Angles To Validate

- Reduced manual work.
- Better operational visibility.
- Fewer errors and handoffs.
- Standardized workflows.
- Faster reporting.
- Improved accountability.
- Better scalability as operations grow.
- Reduced dependency on individual spreadsheet owners.

## 8. Closing Strategy

### Strategy Based On Current Facts

Because the stated blocker is IT security review and the competitor is the spreadsheet status quo, the close strategy should focus on two parallel paths:

1. **Remove the security blocker.**
2. **Strengthen business urgency with the VP Operations.**

### Recommended Close Motion

#### A. Security Critical Path

- Identify IT/security owner.
- Request security checklist and open items.
- Schedule a focused security review call.
- Bring internal security/technical owner.
- Confirm what is required for approval.
- Confirm date by which IT can provide approval or remaining objections.

#### B. Business Case Reinforcement

- Reconfirm the VP Operations’ desired outcome.
- Quantify spreadsheet pain.
- Build a simple value case for the $80k ARR expansion.
- Ask what happens if the team remains on spreadsheets for another quarter.
- Identify operational deadline or initiative tied to this quarter.

#### C. Buying Process Confirmation

Ask VP Operations:

- “Who else needs to approve this expansion?”
- “Is IT security the only remaining blocker?”
- “Does legal, procurement, finance, or vendor onboarding need to review?”
- “What date do we need approval by to close this quarter?”
- “What would prevent this from moving forward?”

#### D. Mutual Close Plan

Use the close plan to make the quarter-close path explicit. If ACME cannot commit to dates or owners, treat the close date as high risk.

## 11. Negotiation Guidance

### Do

- Anchor on the operational cost and risk of the current spreadsheet workflow.
- Keep security review on a named timeline.
- Ask for mutual commitments: ACME provides security requirements and approver path; seller provides approved documentation and timely responses.
- Confirm whether the VP Operations can sign or who else is required.
- Prepare a value-based justification for $80k ARR.

### Do Not

- Do not promise discounts or price concessions.
- Do not guarantee security approval.
- Do not promise legal or contract changes.
- Do not commit to implementation dates without delivery approval.
- Do not claim specific business outcomes without validated data.
- Do not assume the VP Operations is the final signer without confirmation.

### If Price Pressure Appears

Recommended internal response path:

1. Ask what business or budget issue is driving the request.
2. Reconfirm quantified value and urgency.
3. Identify whether scope, timing, or payment structure is the concern.
4. Escalate any concession request to sales leadership.
5. Require approval before making any pricing or contract commitment.
