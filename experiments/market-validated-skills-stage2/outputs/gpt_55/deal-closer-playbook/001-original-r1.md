### Competitor: Incumbent Spreadsheet Workflow

THEIR LIKELY PITCH:  
“Spreadsheets are already in place, free, flexible, familiar, and require no new procurement or security review.”

WHERE THEY WIN:
- No new vendor cost
- No contract process
- Familiar to users
- Flexible and easy to modify
- No change management required
- Already accepted by the organization

WHERE WE WIN:
- Better process control
- Reduced manual work
- Better visibility and reporting
- Improved auditability
- Reduced version-control issues
- Scalable workflows across teams/sites
- More reliable data
- Better leadership visibility

LANDMINE QUESTIONS:

1. “How many people touch the current spreadsheet before the process is complete?”
2. “How do you know everyone is working from the correct version?”
3. “How much time does the team spend reconciling, updating, or chasing spreadsheet inputs each week?”
4. “What happens if an error is found after decisions have already been made?”
5. “If this workflow doubles in volume, does the spreadsheet process still hold up?”
6. “Who owns the process when something breaks — Operations, IT, or individual managers?”

IF THE PROSPECT FAVORS THE STATUS QUO:
- Do not attack spreadsheets.
- Acknowledge they are useful at small scale.
- Quantify the operational cost of staying manual.
- Focus on risk, visibility, and scalability.
- Offer phased rollout to lower change risk.
- If there is no measurable pain, do not force the deal — requalify.

TRAP TO AVOID:
- Do not position the spreadsheet as “bad” or mock the current process.
- The buyer may have built or defended the spreadsheet workflow.
- Position the move as a natural evolution, not a correction of past mistakes.

| Doing nothing | High | No effort required | Avoids ongoing inefficiency and delay | Tie inaction to operational cost |

## 9. Negotiation Strategy

### Our Position

- **Ideal outcome**: $80k ARR expansion signed this quarter, minimal discount, co-termed with existing agreement, standard terms, implementation kickoff scheduled.
- **Acceptable outcome**: $80k ARR or slightly reduced commercial package with clear expansion path, signed this quarter, no material legal/security concessions.
- **Walk-away point**: Heavy discounting or non-standard security/legal commitments without confirmed scope, timeline, or executive approval.

### Concession Strategy

Every concession should be traded for something valuable.

| If They Ask For | We Can Offer | In Exchange For |
|---|---|---|
| Discount | Limited discount, ideally time-bound | Signature by agreed quarter-end date |
| Lower first-year cost | Phased rollout or ramped pricing | Written expansion schedule or multi-year term |
| Extended payment terms | Net 45 or Net 60 if approved internally | Standard legal terms and signed order by date |
| Free implementation | Reduced-scope onboarding package | Case study, reference, or multi-year commitment |
| Additional users / scope | Limited included expansion | Larger committed ARR or renewal extension |
| Security/legal changes | Reasonable documentation and standard DPA | No commercial delay once security clears |

### Red Lines: Do Not Concede

- Unlimited liability
- Unbounded security obligations
- Custom product commitments without internal approval
- Material discounts without closing-date commitment
- Free expansion scope without commercial trade
- Verbal approval without signed paperwork path

### Discount Guidance

Do not discount to beat spreadsheets.  
Instead, quantify the cost of the spreadsheet process.

If discounting is required, position it as tied to:
- Quarter-end signature
- Multi-year commitment
- Faster payment
- Larger expansion
- Customer reference
- Co-terming renewal
