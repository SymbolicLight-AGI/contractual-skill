## 4. Objection Playbook

### Objection 1: “IT security still needs to review this.”

#### Why They Say This

This is a real blocker, not a brush-off. IT is protecting the company from data, compliance, access, vendor, and integration risk. If unmanaged, this can delay signature beyond quarter-end.

#### Response Framework

1. Acknowledge:  
   “That makes sense. Security review is an important step.”

2. Reframe:  
   “The best way to keep this from slowing the business timeline is to run the review in parallel now, not after commercial approval.”

3. Evidence:  
   “For expansion customers, we typically accelerate this by providing the full security packet upfront and scheduling a focused review with the security owner.”

4. Confirm:  
   “If we can resolve security’s questions this week, would that keep us on track for quarter-end?”

#### Example Response

> “Completely understand. Security review should happen before signature. To keep this on track for your quarter-end target, I recommend we start that today instead of waiting until the final order form. We can send our security documentation, data handling details, and any standard questionnaires your team needs. Can you introduce us to the IT security owner so we can schedule a 30-minute working session this week?”

#### If They Push Back

If VP Operations says IT is too busy:

> “Understood. In that case, can we at least get their checklist or questionnaire today? That lets us complete the documentation asynchronously and only use live time for unresolved issues.”

---

### Objection 2: “The spreadsheet workflow works fine for now.”

#### Why They Say This

The spreadsheet process is familiar, cheap, and already adopted. The hidden cost may not be quantified.

#### Response Framework

1. Acknowledge:  
   “Spreadsheets are flexible and they often work well early on.”

2. Reframe:  
   “The question is not whether they work at all. It is whether they still work at the scale and visibility level Operations needs now.”

3. Evidence:  
   “Manual spreadsheet workflows usually create hidden costs in reporting time, version control, rework, and delayed decisions.”

4. Confirm:  
   “Would it be useful if we mapped the current workflow and quantified where time or risk is showing up?”

#### Example Response

> “That makes sense. Spreadsheets are often the fastest way to get a process started. The issue we usually see is that once the workflow becomes business-critical, the spreadsheet starts creating version control issues, manual follow-up, and reporting delays. Rather than assume, let’s quantify it. How many people touch the spreadsheet each week, and how many hours go into updating, reconciling, or reporting from it?”

#### If They Push Back

Offer a low-friction ROI exercise:

> “If the time savings and risk reduction are not meaningful, we should not force the expansion. Let’s spend 20 minutes validating the numbers.”

### Objection 3: “$80k is more than we expected.”

#### Why They Say This

They may not have budget allocated, may not understand ROI, or may be comparing against the apparent zero-cost spreadsheet workflow.

#### Response Framework

1. Acknowledge:

### Competitor: Incumbent Spreadsheet Workflow

#### Their Likely Pitch

The spreadsheet does not actively “pitch,” but the status quo wins by being:

- Already in place
- Familiar to users
- No new vendor review
- No obvious incremental cost
- Flexible for ad hoc changes
- Low perceived implementation burden

#### Where They Win

- Zero procurement friction
- No additional software spend
- Users already know how to use it
- Flexible for one-off exceptions
- No change-management event

#### Where We Win

Assuming your solution addresses the spreadsheet workflow, your strongest positioning is:

- Better visibility for Operations leadership
- Less manual reporting
- Fewer version control issues
- More consistent process execution
- Better audit trail
- Better scalability across teams/sites
- Reduced dependency on individual spreadsheet owners
- More reliable data for decisions

#### Landmine Questions

1. “How many people currently update or depend on the spreadsheet each week?”
2. “What happens when two people are working from different versions?”
3. “How much time does the team spend reconciling, formatting, or checking the data before it is usable?”
4. “If the spreadsheet owner is out for a week, who can confidently run the process?”
5. “What operational decision is delayed because the data is not visible soon enough?”
6. “At what point does this workflow become too important to rely on a manual file?”
7. “What would another quarter of this process cost in team hours?”

#### If the Prospect Favors the Spreadsheet

Do not attack spreadsheets. Position the spreadsheet as a tool that served its purpose but has outgrown the current need.

Recommended response:

> “It sounds like the spreadsheet was the right tool to get the process started. The question is whether it is still the right operating system for this workflow now that the process is more important and more visible. Let’s quantify the current burden and decide based on the numbers.”

#### Trap to Avoid

Do not say, “Spreadsheets are bad.”

That will make users defensive. Instead say:

> “Spreadsheets are great for flexibility. They become risky when the workflow becomes cross-functional, recurring, and business-critical.”
