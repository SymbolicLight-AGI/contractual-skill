# 4. Objection Playbook

Because no objections are known, the rep should prepare for the most likely early-stage objections.

---

## Objection: “We are interested, but we are not ready to move forward yet.”

### Why They Say This

They may not have urgency, budget, internal alignment, or a clearly defined problem.

### Response Framework

1. **Acknowledge:** Validate that timing matters.
2. **Reframe:** Shift from buying now to understanding the cost of waiting.
3. **Evidence:** Use relevant proof once company context is known.
4. **Confirm:** Ask what would need to change for this to become a priority.

### Example Response

> “That makes sense. Timing is usually the hardest part of these decisions. To understand whether it makes sense to keep working on this now or pause, can I ask what happens if nothing changes for the next six months? Is there a business impact, or is this more of a nice-to-have?”

### If They Push Back

Ask:

> “What would make this urgent enough to prioritize?”

---

## Objection: “We do not have budget.”

### Why They Say This

They may not have allocated budget, may not see enough value yet, or the rep may be speaking with someone who does not control spend.

### Example Response

> “Understood. When budget is not already allocated, the question is usually whether the problem is expensive enough to justify creating budget. Can we quantify what this issue is costing today in time, revenue, risk, or operational drag?”

### If They Push Back

Ask who owns budget for the business problem.

---

## Objection: “I need to talk to my boss.”

### Why They Say This

The current contact may be a user, evaluator, or coach — not the economic buyer.

### Example Response

> “Of course. Rather than putting the burden on you to translate everything, would it be useful if we joined that conversation and focused only on the business case, cost of inaction, and decision criteria?”

### If They Push Back

Ask:

> “What will your boss care most about when deciding whether this is worth pursuing?”

---

## Objection: “Send me some information.”

### Why They Say This

This can be a brush-off, or they may need internal material.

### Example Response

> “Happy to. To make sure I send something useful, what are you trying to evaluate — business impact, technical fit, pricing, implementation, or internal approval?”

### If They Push Back

Send a short recap and ask for a specific follow-up date.

# Discovery Script for the Rep

Use this on the next call.

## 1. Set the Frame

> “I want to make sure we are not forcing a sales process where there may not be one. My goal today is to understand whether there is a real business priority, who is involved, and what a useful next step would be.”

## 2. Uncover Pain

> “What prompted the conversation?”  
> “Why is this important now?”  
> “What is the current process or solution?”  
> “What is broken, slow, expensive, risky, or frustrating about it?”

## 3. Quantify Impact

> “How often does this problem happen?”  
> “How many people or teams are affected?”  
> “What does this cost in time, money, risk, or missed opportunity?”  
> “Is there a number leadership already cares about improving?”

## 4. Identify Stakeholders

> “Who owns this problem internally?”  
> “Who would use the solution day to day?”  
> “Who would need to approve budget?”  
> “Who would review technical fit or security?”  
> “Who could block this?”

## 5. Confirm Process

> “If you decided this was worth pursuing, what steps would happen between now and signature?”  
> “Have you bought something like this before?”  
> “Does procurement need to be involved?”  
> “Is legal or security review required?”  
> “Is there an approved budget, or would budget need to be created?”

## 6. Confirm Timing

> “Is there a date this needs to be solved by?”  
> “What happens if that date slips?”  
> “Is this tied to a board meeting, renewal, launch, hiring plan, budget cycle, or executive initiative?”

## 7. Test Champion Strength

> “If you believe this is the right path, are you comfortable helping drive the conversation internally?”  
> “What would you need from us to make that easier?”  
> “Who should we bring into the next meeting?”

## 8. Secure Next Step

> “Based on what we discussed, the best next step is [specific meeting] with [specific people] by [date]. Does that work?”
